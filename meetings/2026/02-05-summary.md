## 2026-02-05 Advisory Board F2F Day 2

What follows is a brief summary of the topics discussed in the W3C Advisory Board F2F hybrid meeting on 2026-02-05

attending: Daniel Appelquist, Hidde de Vries, Wei Ding, Emma Fraser, Max Gendler, Tatsuya Igarashi, Elena Lape, An Qi (Angel) Li, Coralie Mercier, Theresa O'Connor, Hiroshi Ota, Avneesh Singh, Amy van der Hiel, Brent Zundel

* **Incubation of new work**
DingWei gave a presentation about the Exploration IG including feedback from members and ideas of how to make the IG more successful. He presented the idea of an Exploration Ambassador Program to liaise with other peer organizations  (including open source) to track ideas related to the web and web technology. He suggested that programs for Exploration Ambassador Roundtables could be part of some Monthly Member meetings to allow Ambassadors to report back to the Membership. He noted that to be successful, the role of Ambassador would have to be appealing for people to take on. There were some questions about the official status of such a role and how it would be seen externally. It was noted the W3C already has Liaisons to peer organizations which are acknowledged as an official role and it's important that any new project not undermine the liaison or their credibility. It was agreed that the Ambassador role as a bridge between organizations to report back would have to be clearly communicated both to any potential Ambassador and to outside organizations. It was suggested that with existing Liaisons that there might be less need for an Ambassador and that someone who was interested might simply attend the meetings and report back. It was proposed that there could be periodic calls for people who are involved in other SDOs, including AB members, to report back to Membership. DingWei noted he will refine the proposal and bring it back to the AB.

* **Process Refactoring Breakouts: WG Evolution**
The AB broke into 3 groups to discuss questions raised on Process refactoring based on WG feedback at TPAC. The first group looked at the charter template. They wanted to ask Team to identify charters that diverge from the template. They said when doing review it's important to gauge whether the group seems to be engaged and doing the work well. They noted that there are some glitches in the diffs in the new charter template and that a charter should clearly highlight why it is different and how.

The second group looked at what charters provide and why they are necessary. They noted that charters provide: an idea of resources, team effort; expectations for strategic planning and publications; and help give an idea of the scope and deliverable of the work which allows people to know what is happening at W3C.

The third group looked at how re-chartering might evolve. They noted that there seem to be three types of charters: 1. continue as is. 2. haven't finished work, shifted or changing; and  3. needs more time (extension). They suggested that the process could be simplified so that if a group is just extending they could ask for objections but otherwise move forward. The noted that for some linked CGs moving to WGs, that if the scope stays the same, they might also ask for objections but otherwise move forward. They noted that for FOs it might be good to allow a group to keep working even when there's a council. For charter extensions it was suggested there could be a timeframe grace (eg: up to 6 months, ok) after which the group would have to show reasonable activity.  

The idea of a dashboard of all charters was raised. It was also noted that review seems appropriate when a group adds new deliverables. It was noted that while the aim is to make the Process more simple and lessen burdens on groups, that the process does need some friction, some check in points, so that people pay attention and engage. The uneven cadence of horizontal review was noted and that  sometimes review is done so late that groups can't easily incorporate feedback. It was mentioned that it's understood that some groups are doing more maintenance than chasing new ideas and that some groups end up needing to be restarted again. It was suggested that groups might want a way to continue on with a periodic review of their functions - though it was also noted that rechartering does give an opportunity to see interest/commitment by those who rejoin and helps insure that it is work that Members prioritize. Brent and Tess noted they would add the points of discussions as Issues in the Process repo and Charter repo for review.

* **Engagement Groups**
Coralie joined the AB to talk about Engagement (previously Affinity) Groups again. She reiterated the idea of Team identifying groups of members by topic, interest, region etc in order to make connections, share information and increase member engagement and to turn "noise into signal". She noted such groups would be flexible, not formal or chartered like WG/IG. She gave the example of some members gathering after a meeting discuss a promising idea and that interest might grow from there. Hidde noted that within a11y there are different  groups but there is no overarching WAI group and something like that could be interesting. Brent likened the idea being asked to identify your interests when you start a service, eg: digital identity, css, networking, leadership. He noted such interests might be used by Team for outreach to people interested a topic.He also noted if there were a charter on digital identity, if 80% of the people tagged as interested in digital identity vote, then it shows people are engaged. Elena noted in Marketing this is called 'tribes" and it's a vital way to understand customers/an audience. AB members discussed this as being like a tailored membership experience and potentially good for generating new ideas, getting people together and allowing members to know who else to engage with in a positive way. AB members noted this would be helpful for finding peers or identifying where ideas are being discussed. It was clarified that while W3C is made of members, having individual participants be the target for engagement groups made sense (though a member might gain insight by seeing what groups, what tag cluster of engagements, their participants are involved in). One participant might want to be in an engagement group on accessibility to know what's going even if they were not in a WG/IG on the topic. Coralie confirmed that this would not be a change that would lead to withholding or siloing of information from members, but another means for additional engagement. The AB encouraged both grouping by Team and options for participants to self-describe. The AB was interested in the idea and supports efforts for member engagement.

* **Process Refactoring Breakouts: Tooling**
The AB broke into groups again for breakouts on tooling in Process refactoring on three topics: a horizontal review dashboard; Automatic scribing; and Disposition of Comments. The Breakout Group on a horizontal review dashboard identified stakeholders for such a dashboard (spec and document owners, horizontal reviewers, AC reps, Team and the wider community)  and noted that such a dashboard  would form one single place all stakeholder could monitor the review cycle. The Breakout Group on Automatic scribing discussed potential features such as the ability to create minutes, access control, adding comments, pausing transcription and deleting recordings after minutes are finalized. Breakout Group C discussed potential automated tooling for Disposition of Comments in GH noting the importance of oversight and thoroughness. The AB discussed the three topics and noted that horizontal review groups each seemed to have their own process which could sometimes make it difficult for groups to engage. They also discussed that automatic minutes might lead to mistakes or misrepresentations so would need to be consistently checked by groups. The AB noted that dealing with public comments in the disposition of comments is necessary for legitimizing the work of W3C.

The AB acknowledged that while the utility of any tooling is interesting, the burden shouldn't fall entirely on Team and that all tools take time and effort to maintain. OS projects were mentioned as an option (though not a magic wand).  It was noted sometimes participants in Groups have created very useful tools. It was emphasized that if tools are developed that it's important to take accessibility needs into account from the start. Hackathons and getting support from member employees were mentioned as was finding tools that already exist and the idea of an open source tools CG/WG was suggested.
